\begin{abstract}
    This work studies deep reinforcement learning for continuous control from visual observations using MuJoCo environments from the DeepMind Control Suite accessed through Gymnasium. The focus is on Actor-Critic algorithms with explicit policy approximation: Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), an on-policy method, and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), an off-policy maximum-entropy method. We first select and configure a locomotion environment with RGB observations and discretized actions. Then, we train PPO and SAC agents for forward locomotion, analyzing the impact of reward design, visual state representation and hyperparameters on learning dynamics. We experimentally compare PPO and SAC in terms of sample efficiency, training stability and final performance. The results highlight the practical trade-offs between on-policy and off-policy Actor-Critic methods in visually rich continuous control, as well as the role of entropy regularization and other components in shaping the learning process.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In this practice we address continuous control problems from the perspective of Actor-Critic methods with explicit policy parameterization. Unlike in the previous assignment, where the policy was implicit and derived from an approximated action-value function $Q(s,a)$, here the agent learns a stochastic policy $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ together with a value function used as critic. The state of the environment is provided exclusively through RGB images, so the agent must simultaneously learn a visual representation and a control policy on top of it. [file:387]

The physical simulation is handled by MuJoCo, a physics engine widely used in research on locomotion and manipulation, while the tasks are defined via the DeepMind Control Suite and exposed through Gymnasium, which provides a unified RL API. In this project we implement and/or adapt two representative Actor-Critic algorithms: Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), an on-policy method based on a clipped surrogate objective and Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE), and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), an off-policy method based on the maximum entropy RL framework with replay buffer, double critic and target networks. [file:387]

The goals of the practice are: (i) to understand theoretically and empirically how PPO and SAC work in continuous control with visual observations, (ii) to configure and train agents in locomotion environments, (iii) to compare on-policy vs off-policy behaviour in terms of sample efficiency, stability and final performance, and (iv) to extend the analysis to a more demanding setting where the agent must both move forward and avoid obstacles. The report is structured according to the four parts specified in the assignment: PPO locomotion, SAC locomotion, comparative analysis, and locomotion with obstacles, followed by general conclusions.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Analysis}

Before diving into the empirical results, we think it would be optimal to gather our thoughts around what we think are the key theoretical aspects of PPO that might influence its performance in the locomotion task. This will be even more relevant when we add the later comparison with SAC, as it will allow us to better understand the differences between on-policy and off-policy methods in this context.

The first aspect to consider is the nature of the policy update in PPO. The clipped surrogate objective is designed to prevent large updates that could destabilize training, which marks its main improvement with respect to earlier on-policy methods like TRPO, as it deals with the issue of instability in the policy gradents. However, this also means that PPO might be more conservative in its updates, which could lead to slower learning in some cases, especially in environments with sparse rewards or where significant changes in the policy are needed to make progress.

Related to this last point, PPO is on-policy. As we have seen over and over during the lectures, this means that the data used for training the policy is generated by the current policy itself. The main advantage of this is that it simplifies the learning process and can lead to more stable updates, as the data distribution is always aligned with the current policy (bias-variance trade-off). However, it implies a much lower sample efficiency compared to off-policy methods, as the data cannot be reused across multiple updates.

Due to this, we expect that PPO will require a larger number of interactions with the environment to achieve good performance, which could be a significant drawback in more complex environments or when computational resources are limited. On the other hand, it might be more robust to hyperparameter choices and less sensitive to the quality of the value function approximation, as the policy updates are more constrained (which can be understood as a simpler training, as only longer computation times are needed, but not necessarily more careful tuning).

\subsubsection{Empirical Results}

The full training of the PPO agent in the Hopper environment with RGB observations was done through the equivalent of 10 days of training (around $3\times 10^6$ environment episodes), which is a significant computational effort (as we will see, we were not able to train the SAC agent for the same duration, since the deadline pressed us to prioritize the Report writing). Despite this, the training reward peaked a little before the million$^{th}$ episode, which tells us that PPO can learn a good locomotion policy in this environment in 3-4 days of training. At this point, the reward was around 2720, which is a good performance for this task (as we can also see in the visualization of the trained agent, which shows a stable forward locomotion, reaching the end of the track with no falls). Figure \ref{fig:ppo_graph} visualizes these results, showing the learning curves for the reward, actor and critic losses, and the evolution of the entropy coefficient (which is automatically adjusted during training). The reward curve shows a clear upward trend until it peaks, while the actor and critic losses show a more noisy behaviour, which is expected given the nature of the updates in PPO. The entropy coefficient starts at a high value to encourage exploration and then decreases as the policy converges to a more deterministic behaviour.

Something to note about the training is that the mean reward curve collapsed after reaching the peak, so we have only taken into account the results obtained up to that point.

In the case of PPO, the hyperparameters didn't require such a careful tuning as in the case of SAC, which is consistent with our theoretical analysis. We found that the most critical hyperparameters were those related to the reward design, although not as much as in the case of SAC (which we will discuss in the next section). The learning rate and the clipping parameter were not as sensitive, and we were able to find a good configuration with a few trials.

This raises several questions about the stability of PPO in this task, as well as about the role of the reward design in shaping the learning process. Even though we were able to devote a substantial amount of time to the training, it would have been interesting to explore more systematically the impact of different reward components and their weights, to see whether optimal attitudes were achieved in less time or with less computational resources by a different combination of reward hyperparameters than the ones used \footnote{These were $\text{forward\_reward\_weight}=0.5$, $\text{healthy\_reward}=1.25$, $\text{ctrl\_cost\_weight}=0.1$.}.

Some key observations we can make about the training process are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sample efficiency}: As expected, PPO required a large number of episodes to reach good performance, which is a known limitation of on-policy methods. Early training showed a high variance in results (with actor/critic losses fluctuating significantly).
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter sensitivity}: PPO was relatively robust to changes in learning rate and clipping parameter, as well as to reward component weights, although the latter still had a significant impact on the learning dynamics and final performance.
    \item \textbf{Stability}: The training was generally stable, with a clear upward trend in rewards until the peak, although the collapse after the peak raises questions about potential overfitting or instability in later stages of training.
    \item \textbf{Behavioral analysis}: The trained agent showed a stable forward locomotion, which indicates that PPO was able to learn a good policy for this task, although the visual observation space might have made the learning process more challenging (a difficulty that was surpassed by means of more training time).
\end{itemize}

Ideas:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The main trouble while training arose from the election of the hyperparameters. Specifically, those related to the rewards:
          \begin{enumerate}
              \item \textbf{forward\_reward\_weight}: This is the reward component that encourages the agent to move forward. If this weight is too low, the agent might not learn to prioritize forward movement, which is essential for locomotion. On the other hand, if it's too high, the agent might focus solely on moving forward without learning to balance or avoid obstacles effectively. With respect to our environment (Hopper), we found that a too high forward reward weight led to the agent leaning forward in excess and falling prematurely, instead of learning a more balanced dynamic.
              \item \textbf{healthy\_reward}: This reward component encourages the agent to maintain a healthy state which means, in our task, to keep the torso upright and avoid colliding against the floor with a body part other than the foot. If this reward is too low, the agent might not get the correct balance and just try to go forward aimlessly, which results in short episodes of forward movement followed by a fall. Conversely, if it's too high, the agent might learn to prioritize staying upright over moving forward. This showed intensely in our training, as relatively high values (which we tuned by trial and error, thus being unsure about the exact threshold) led to the agent obtaining high rewards (in the thousands) by just standing still in its upright position, without even trying to take a single step forward.
              \item \textbf{ctrl\_cost\_weight}: This is the penalty for taking actions, which encourages the agent to find more efficient and smoother movements. If this weight is too high, the agent might learn to take very small actions to minimize the cost, which can lead to very slow learning or suboptimal policies. Conversely, if it's too low, the agent might take erratic and large actions that could lead to instability. We are not yet sure how this parameter affected our training (as it surely depends on its relative relationship with the two other reward components), as even low values led to the agent standing still in its upright position, which is a local optimum in the reward landscape. This might indicate that the control cost was not high enough to encourage the agent to explore more dynamic movements, or that the forward reward was not sufficiently incentivizing forward movement.
          \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}
